# CV Bullet Construction Guide

Reference document for writing strong, defensible CV bullets at both leadership (Associate Director+) and IC levels. Load at Phase 4 content generation, before writing experience bullets.

---

## Core Framework: CCAR

The CCAR framework is the standard for leadership-level bullets and a strong baseline for IC bullets.

| Component | Definition | Common Failure Mode |
|---|---|---|
| **Context** | Organizational situation, what was inherited, scale of scope | Omitted entirely — reader cannot calibrate significance |
| **Challenge** | The specific problem, gap, or risk that required action | Understated — makes outcomes look routine |
| **Action** | The leadership approach and key decisions made | Over-detailed on tasks rather than decisions |
| **Result** | What changed — structurally, operationally, or strategically | Generic category label instead of specific change |

For IC bullets, CCAR still applies but Context and Challenge are often compressed into a single clause. For leadership bullets, all four components are expected.

---

## What Distinguishes Leadership Bullets from IC Bullets

| Dimension | IC | Leadership (AD+) |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | What I did; tasks and project outputs | Why it mattered strategically; enterprise or portfolio outcomes |
| **Scope** | Departmental or project-level | Organizational, cross-functional, portfolio, or multi-year |
| **Verb choice** | Developed, managed, supported, implemented | Championed, orchestrated, spearheaded, aligned, redesigned |
| **Outcome type** | Metrics tied to specific deliverable | Capability shift, risk reduction, structural change, strategic enablement |
| **Time horizon** | Immediate or project-level | Multi-year, program-level, or career-spanning |
| **Scale signal** | Team or project size | Portfolio breadth, budget, number of studies/vendors/functions affected |

A useful test: IC bullets explain *what*. Leadership bullets explain *why it was strategically significant* and anchor to organizational objectives or capabilities gained.

---

## Scale Indicators — Critical for Leadership Level

Scale establishes complexity and seniority before the outcome is even read. It belongs early in the bullet — in the Context or Challenge component.

Priority scale signals to surface:
- **Portfolio or program scope** — number of studies, programs, therapeutic areas
- **Vendor or partner scope** — number of external partners managed or governed
- **Team scope** — direct reports, matrix team members, global reach
- **Budget scope** — where known or approximately known
- **Regulatory or compliance scope** — number of audits, frameworks governed, submission exposure
- **Organizational reach** — number of functions affected, global vs. regional scope

Scale does not require hard numbers to be useful. "Spanning 5 studies and 7+ vendors simultaneously" is specific enough to establish complexity without precise headcount or budget data.

## Impact Statement Types

Use the strongest type available. Do not fabricate metrics. Do not default to vague category labels.

### Type 1: Quantified
Hard numbers where genuinely known.
> "Reduced laboratory data acquisition rework from ~50% to 0% across all governed studies."
> "Narrowed preferred-partner laboratory field from ~40 candidates to 3."

### Type 2: Bounded Qualitative
Specific, verifiable claims without precise metrics. "First," "only," "eliminated," "established" — observable and defensible.
> "Built the first governed data exchange process in the function — no equivalent existed prior."
> "Established the only cross-functional data review governance framework across clinical, data management, and biostatistics."

### Type 3: Contextual Narrative
Before-state + after-state. Explains what existed before and what changed. Verifiable through references.
> "Inherited a function with no governed quality planning infrastructure and submission rework as the standard correction mechanism. Redesigned from ground up — established CDASH-aligned check library, accountability frameworks, and technology alignment."

Type 3 is the most underused and the most powerful for governance and process work, where hard metrics rarely exist but the structural change is unambiguous.

## Governance, Process, and Capability-Building Work

These are legitimate leadership accomplishments — increasingly recognized as strategic risk management and organizational capability building (2024+ practitioner consensus). They are *more defensible* than outcome metrics because the structure is observable.

Defensibility comes from:
- **What structure existed before** (often nothing, or inconsistent)
- **What was established** (specific artifacts: frameworks, libraries, SOPs, governance bodies)
- **How broadly it applied** (scope: studies, vendors, functions, geographies)
- **What risk or inefficiency it addressed** (the business case for why it mattered)

Avoid: "Led redesign of data quality processes." (action label, no context or outcome)
Use: "Inherited inconsistent, study-by-study quality planning with no cross-study accountability structure. Redesigned the initiation process, created a CDASH-aligned quality check library, and established accountability frameworks — materially reducing mid-study volatility and submission rework." (context + action + specific outcome)

## When Quantitative Data Is Not Available

In order of preference:
1. **Bounded qualitative** — specific structural claim that is verifiable ("first," "only," "eliminated")
2. **Contextual narrative** — before-state and after-state with scope
3. **Proxy metrics** — adoption rate, timeline compression, audit outcomes, incident reduction, rework elimination
4. **Scope as signal** — when outcome is genuinely unmeasurable, scale of scope establishes significance

Never use vague category labels as impact statements: "improved efficiency," "enhanced quality," "reduced risk." These are tags, not bullets.

## Inventory Annotation Requirements

For the Experience Inventory to support strong bullet writing, each high-priority entry should carry:

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| `Impact:` | Specific outcome — quantified, bounded qualitative, or contextual narrative |
| `Scale:` (optional, embed in Impact if cleaner) | Portfolio, team, vendor, or program scope |
| `Context:` | What was inherited, what was missing, what the organizational problem was |

The `Context:` field already exists on a small number of entries. The `Impact:` field exists on a small number. The annotation work is to extend both systematically across the director-era BioMarin entries.
