## 1. Databricks Enterprise Selection

    -   pre-built clinical data functionality with lower initial development burden,

    -   lower upfront risk, and

    -   no dependency on enterprise adoption decisions

    -   synergy with existing processes

-   The cost of those options was a scalability ceiling: they solved the immediate problem but not the growth problem, and their cost model would continue to sit inside the DMS budget regardless of organizational scale.

-   Databricks offered the highest scalability, an enterprise cost distribution model that removed dedicated budget burden from DMS, and downstream synergies with clinical and statistical processes that none of the other platforms could match.

-   The cost was real: capabilities would need to be built from scratch rather than configured, Databricks entered the evaluation late leaving limited time for a thorough proof-of-concept, and adoption required organizational alignment that was not guaranteed.

-   Secondary criteria was scalability without proportional headcount growth, which was the stated organizational constraint.

-   Tertiary was cost model alignment with enterprise direction and forward-looking capability for AI readiness and downstream process synergies

-   Solving the immediate problem at the expense of the growth problem was not an acceptable outcome.

### [My Reasoning]{.underline} 

-   The off-the-shelf platforms were adequate answers to the current state.

-   Databricks was the only platform positioned to remain adequate as the organization scaled.

-   The conditional framing was deliberate and honest --- recommending Databricks unconditionally would have overstated confidence in a variable I did not control.

-   If enterprise adoption did not happen, the cost distribution argument collapsed and a different recommendation would have been correct.

### [The Outcome]{.underline}

-   A three-phase modernization roadmap was developed covering initial ingestion and conformance capabilities, domain and modality expansion, and AI-enabled decision capabilities.

### [What I\'d Own Differently]{.underline}

-   For a company that requires demonstrated value before moving, a platform selection without a concrete use case roadmap designed to build organizational confidence was insufficient.

-   The Python greenfield build carried sunk cost risk, but in hindsight I knew where the industry was going and I knew where the organization needed to go.

-   Building in Python would have sent the right signal, maintained forward momentum on the actual future state, and the delivery continuity risk was probably manageable.

-   The resistance I encountered when trying to move away from SAS later was partly a problem I created by using SAS as the bridge.

-   When you have a clear view of where something needs to end up, building the interim state in the wrong direction has compounding costs that aren\'t always visible at the time of the decision.
